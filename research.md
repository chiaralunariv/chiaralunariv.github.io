## Research interests

My research investigates language production and language processing in the context of deafness. This research
program is developed through several complementary approaches:

• Sign language production: Linguistic information in sign language is conveyed through the simultaneous
movement of multiple facial and bodily articulators. I use different motion tracking systems to measure the visual
signal of sign language and extract relevant kinematic features. I am particularly interested in how physical
properties of the signal relate to specific linguistic features, such as iconicity and syllabic structure. I apply
motion tracking analysis both to narrative production, using a quasi-naturalistic dataset of spoken and signed
language videos that I developed at BCBL, and to isolated lexical signs from the LSE-Sign dataset
(Gutierrez-Sigut et al., 2016).

• Sign language processing: I investigate how the temporal structure of sign language shapes cognitive and neural
processing in comparison to spoken language. Unlike speech, which unfolds largely sequentially over time, sign
language conveys linguistic information simultaneously across multiple articulators. In an intelligibility
experiment, I showed that this structure makes sign language more resilient to temporal distortion, highlighting
the importance of spatial dimensions in language processing (Rivolta et al., 2021). Building on these findings, I
conducted an MEG study on cortical tracking during sign language comprehension (Rivolta et al., 2025). The
results showed that cortical tracking is an amodal mechanism engaged in both spoken and signed language
processing, although its frequency profile and neural topography vary across modalities. Moreover, tracking was
modulated by language knowledge, and its strength varied across articulators according to their linguistic
relevance. A second MEG study is currently underway to extend these findings to deaf signers and to more
fine-grained sign language movements.

• Reading in deafness: Deaf individuals often struggle to achieve high reading proficiency due to reduced access to
auditory phonology and socio-educational factors. I coordinated a large multi-technique project combining
behavioral measures, eye-tracking, MEG, and fMRI to investigate the cognitive and neural basis of phonological
and orthographic processing in highly skilled deaf readers. The project leverages two well-established effects in
the reading literature, the pseudohomophone effect and the transposed-letter effect, to examine whether skilled
deaf readers access phonological and orthographic information similarly to reading-matched hearing peers or rely
on alternative strategies.
